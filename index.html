REACH

  Research Culture Week

    16-20 November 2020

Research is more than just the creation of new knowledge.


The process is deeply intertwined with the values, standards, and expectations of our academic communities. These norms fundamentally affect our personal wellbeing, the way we both approach and choose the problems we want to solve, which voices in the community are heard---and the course of our careers.


How can our research communities become fairer and healthier places to work and thrive? Join us for ten important discussions with leading and local experts on how this vision can be achieved.

    David Sampson
    Research Culture – A Personal Journey

    We exist in a research culture created in the post-war boom that generated the wealth and resources to pursue knowledge in universities for its own sake in what was a cottage industry. The rarefied atmosphere of the 1960s and 70s has successively given way to the massification of higher education, including research, and the pursuit of knowledge for its own sake has given way to greater expectations on the contributions of our research to society. Have our structures, expectations and culture moved with these changes? We may not still use paper and typewriter, but much else has remained the same. Through the lens of my own personal journey spanning five decades (not completely!), precious conversations with colleagues, and interactions with inspiring students, I will consider how we got here, where we need to go, what we need to keep, and what we need to change, to continue to make academia the place where we all want to be.
  • 4.00 PM
    Kate Hext
    Reach for the Sky: Thinking Globally (on a Budget), Plotting your Career (in a Pandemic)

    How can you network effectively and create opportunities to take you beyond your PhD, and into a successful academic career? The answers to these questions have always been essential, but they are more important than ever now, in the wake of Covid and seismic shifts to higher education in the UK. In the future, academics will have to be pragmatic and enterprising as never before. This talk looks at the benefits to building a rich international research community. It looks at some of the opportunities, including new avenues for collaboration opened by the 'shift online' caused by Covid, and how best to engage with these to enhance your work and build your career.

    Jim Al-Khalili
    Spreading the Word: Why Science Communication is so Vital

    Science communication has changed out of all recognition over the past quarter of a century that I have been actively involved in it. In this talk I will discuss how and why science communication, particularly in the UK, has undergone such a revolution. I will also say something about the way science and scientists have been perceived by the general public during the coronavirus pandemic. We hear terms like 'following the science' or 'heeding the advice of experts', but what do they really mean? So, what has changed and is it a change for the better? I will also address the wider issues surrounding the importance of communicating science to society.
  • 4.00 PM
    Mirjana Povic
    Science for Development in Africa

    Due to natural (such as its dark skies) and human resources, Africa has great potential for scientific research in astronomy and space science. At the same time, the continent still faces many difficulties and its countries now recognise the importance of sciences such as astronomy, space science, and satellite technologies for improving some of its main socio-economic and ecological challenges. Development in astronomy and space science in Africa has grown significantly over the past few years, and it has been never more possible to use astronomy for development than it is now. However, much remains to be done. This talk will summarise recent developments in astronomy in Africa and using Ethiopia as an example will show how working together in development of science and education we can fight poverty on a long-term and increase in future our chances of achieving the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals for the benefit of our entire society.

    Osama Khan
    Mind the Gap

    I will share my experience of being a Bangladeshi Muslim immigrant in the academy in the UK alongside my life as a British citizen. I will reflect on how at times I struggled with my multiple identities and how I have decided to deal with them. I will share the awarding gap that exists in the UK universities and how Surrey compares. The talk will also reflect on the concept of glass ceiling, more like concrete ceiling, for academic and professional services careers in the UK academy. Finally, we will reflect together what would be the way forward.
  • 6.00 PM
    Angela Saini
    Facts and Narratives in Research

    As academic institutions seek to explain and fix their enormous gaps in race and gender pay and promotion, and as social justice movements work to address these issues more widely in society, what are researchers doing to build narratives around the reasons for the inequality we see? Science journalist Angela Saini argues that science remains shot through with racism and sexism, affecting not only how institutions work, but also the work they produce. We need to have more accurate facts about human difference and the causes of inequality, but - more urgently - we need to explain them better.

    How to Master your Interview

    In today’s climate, securing the next career opportunity, both within academia and in industry, is going to become more and more competitive. In this session, Will Davies and Tony Simmonds will discuss some of the key considerations that anyone attending an interview as a candidate should bear in mind, as well as sharing some useful tips that might help secure that interview in the first place.   • 4.00 PM
    Derk-Jan Dijk
    Sleep Sufficiency, Stress, and Success

    Sleep is a major determinant of physical and mental health. Insufficient sleep leads to sleepiness, impaired performance, changes in mood, increased appetite, altered immune function, and a wide range of other behavioural, physiological and molecular changes. Some of the major processes regulating sleep, and factors leading to insufficient sleep have been identified. Understanding the factors contributing to insufficient sleep and implementing behavioural and environmental changes to remove these factors or reduce their impact on sleep is an efficient way to improve health, well-being and the likelihood of success.
